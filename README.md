## The Central Finding

> A VGG-16 model trained on mammographic images achieved **98% accuracy**, **99% precision**, and **97% MCC**.  
> Grad-CAM was applied to generate spatially resolved heat maps of each model decision.  
> These outputs were validated against expert radiologists (average experience: **9.7 years**).  
> **Only 30% of radiologists correctly identified the disease** from the AI outputs — despite 98% model accuracy.

This result — that clinical trustworthiness and statistical accuracy are not the same thing — is the core contribution of this research.

---

## Research Overview

### Problem
Deep learning models for breast cancer diagnosis achieve high benchmark accuracy but lack interpretability. Clinicians cannot trust or act on outputs they cannot understand.

### Approach
1. Trained three CNN architectures (CNN baseline, ResNet-50, VGG-16) on the MIAS mammography dataset (322 images)
2. Applied **Grad-CAM** (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping) to generate spatially resolved heat maps of model decisions
3. Conducted a formal **clinical validation survey** with expert radiologists using the CHF scale and XAI Explanation Satisfaction scale

### Key Results

| Model | Accuracy | Precision | Sensitivity | Specificity | MCC |
|-------|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| CNN (Baseline) | 96% | 95% | 97% | 95% | 91% |
| ResNet-50 | 97% | 98% | 97% | 98% | 95% |
| **VGG-16** | **98%** | **99%** | **98%** | **99%** | **97%** |

### Clinical Validation Survey Results

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Radiologists surveyed | 10 |
| Average age | 35.6 years |
| Average experience | 9.7 years |
| Prior AI experience | 20% |
| Correctly identified disease from AI output | **30%** |
| Could not identify disease from AI output | **70%** |

## Dataset

**MIAS (Mammographic Image Analysis Society) Database**
- 322 mammograms (161 bilateral pairs)
- 54 malignant, 67 benign, 201 normal
- 7 abnormality categories: CIRC, SPIC, NORM, MISC, ARCH, CALC, ASYM
- Data augmentation applied: 322 → 1028 images (flip + rotation)
- Train/test split: 75% / 25%

## XAI Survey Scales Used

- **CHF Scale** (Cahour-Forzy): Measures AI system trustworthiness, predictability, dependability, and efficiency
- **XAI Explanation Satisfaction Scale**: Measures whether AI explanations support clinical decision-making

**CHF Scale mean: 4.30 | XAI Satisfaction mean: 3.76**

Regression analysis: CHF scale explains 50% of variance in XAI Explanation Satisfaction (Adjusted R² = 0.50, β = 0.85)

---

## Clinical Implication

The gap between a model's statistical accuracy and its clinical utility is not a technical problem — it is an epistemological one. A model that computes correctly but produces outputs that clinicians cannot interpret or trust is not a clinical tool. Closing this gap is the research question this thesis raised and the problem the author is pursuing at doctoral level.
